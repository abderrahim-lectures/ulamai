\section{Ring Theory: Every PID is a UFD}

\begin{theorem}[pid\_is\_ufd]
Every principal ideal domain $R$ is a unique factorization domain: every
nonzero nonunit element of $R$ factors into irreducibles, and this
factorization is unique up to order and associates.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Existence.} Suppose some nonzero nonunit $a \in R$ has no
factorization into irreducibles. Since $R$ is a PID it is Noetherian
(every ascending chain of ideals stabilizes, as a union of a chain of
ideals is an ideal, hence principal, hence generated by some element of
some ideal in the chain). If $a$ is not irreducible, write $a = a_1 b_1$
with neither a unit; at least one factor, say $a_1$, also has no
irreducible factorization, giving a strictly increasing chain of ideals
$(a) \subsetneq (a_1) \subsetneq (a_2) \subsetneq \cdots$, contradicting the
ascending chain condition.

\textbf{Uniqueness.} In a PID, every irreducible element is prime: if $p$
is irreducible and $p \mid xy$, consider the ideal $(p, x)$; since $R$ is a
PID, $(p,x) = (d)$ for some $d$, and $d \mid p$ forces $d$ to be a unit or
an associate of $p$. If $d$ is a unit, $1 = rp + sx$ for some $r,s$, so
$y = rpy + sxy$ is divisible by $p$; if $d$ is an associate of $p$, then
$p \mid x$. Either way $p \mid x$ or $p \mid y$. Given two irreducible
factorizations of $a$, primeness lets us match factors one by one (up to
associates) by induction on the number of factors, proving uniqueness.
\end{proof}
